The Gray Clan of Scotland

Origins and Early History

The Gray family, or Clan Gray, traces its roots to the 13th century in Scotland, with origins potentially linked to the town of Gray in Haute-Saône, France. Fulbert de Gray, who served as the Great Chamberlain to Robert, Duke of Normandy, was among the earliest noted bearers of the name. It is believed he received lands in Croy, Picardy, adopting "Gray" as his surname.

Key Figures and Early Mentions:

  • Hugo de Gray (1248): The first recorded Gray in Scotland, a witness in a charter by Walter de Lundin.

  • John Gray (1250-1266): Mayor of Berwick, witnessed a land gift to the Hospital of Soltre.

  • Henry Gray of Fife (1296): Paid homage to Edward I of England, documented in the Ragman Rolls.

  • John Gray of Broxmouth (1357): Received a charter for the lands of Craigy.

  • Ibbote Gray (1376): Leased the land of Molyne.

Role in the Wars of Independence

The Grays initially pledged allegiance to Edward I but later supported Scotland's fight for independence under Robert the Bruce. Sir Andrew Gray, a significant figure during this period, played a notable role in military campaigns and was rewarded with land grants, including holdings at Longforgan in Tayside.

Historical Titles and Events

  • Ragman Rolls (1296): Like many other Scottish families, the Grays swore loyalty to Edward I, a move that was later reversed in support of Scottish sovereignty.

  • Lord Gray Lineage: In modern times, Angus Diarmid Ian Campbell-Gray, the 22nd Lord Gray, was barred from the chiefship by the Lord Lyon's 1950 ruling due to a double-barrelled name.

Crest, Motto, and Tartan

The Gray Clan's traditions are rich with symbols and regalia:

  • Crest: An anchor, symbolizing steadfastness.

  • Motto: "Anchor, Fast Anchor."

  • Tartans:

    • Gray Hunting Ancient: A rare, historical tartan with a camouflage pattern.

    • Gray Personal Tartan (1986): Designed by M.G. Gray or Mrs. R. Gray of Alloa.

    • Claret & Green: Inspired by the Stuart of Bute tartan.

Clan Affiliations and Septs

The Gray family is sometimes considered part of the Septs of Clan Stewart or Clan Sutherland, reflecting the interconnectedness of Scottish clans.

Notable Events and Achievements

  • John Gray (1327): Served as Provost of Crail.

  • William dictus Gray (1333-1363): Witnessed a charter by Muriel, widow of Sir William de Roe.

  • Sir Andrew Gray: His valor in battle earned him lands and prestige, reinforcing the Gray family's status.

Modern Legacy

Today, the name "Gray" is relatively common throughout Scotland, with a deep history marked by loyalty, strategic alliances, and contributions to the country's legacy of independence and nobility.
